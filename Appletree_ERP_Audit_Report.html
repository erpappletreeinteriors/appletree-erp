Executive Summary

This is a genuinely unusual artifact: a full operational ERP — CRM, parametric costing, procurement, factory MES, HR, and finance — built and deployed as one continuously-evolving internal tool rather than bought off a shelf. That origin is both the biggest strength and the central risk on this desk.

Biggest strengths

  • End-to-end coverage: a lead can flow to quotation, costed BOQ, project, procurement, factory job, site install, invoice and P&L inside one system — no spreadsheet handoffs.
  • A genuine parametric costing engine (geometry → material/hardware/labour → margin) most competitors in this price bracket don't have; it's the single most defensible piece of IP here.
  • Role-based access is real and CEO-editable at runtime, not hardcoded — unusually mature for a bootstrapped build.
  • Fast iteration cycle: five major modules shipped, tested, and deployed in a single engineering cycle with zero regressions.

Biggest weaknesses

  • Zero client-facing surface. No portal, no e-signature, no online payment — the entire system is staff-only, which caps both CX and marketing maturity scores hard.
  • No AI, no WhatsApp/email/SMS automation, no ad-platform integration anywhere in the stack — the marketing and automation layers are essentially unbuilt.
  • Single-file architecture and a generic JSONB-per-table data model will start fighting the business well before 5–10 concurrent users or serious BI needs.
  • Single-tenant, single-org data model — not multi-branch-ready without real schema work, which matters the moment a second city opens.

Highest-priority finance gap

That's fine at current scale but will not pass a real audit and should be flagged to any investor or auditor before diligence, not discovered during it.

Interior Design Workflow Analysis

This is the honest gap between "costing tool" and "design tool" — the ERP prices a design extremely well, but does not participate in producing one.

Supported

  • BOQ generation (from both manual room/item entry and the parametric engine)
  • Material selection at the costing-line level (core, finish, edge-band, hardware)
  • Client approval workflow (quotation status: Sent → Approved/Won)
  • Change orders in effect via quotation revisioning

Not supported

  • Concept design, mood boards, 3D visualization
  • CAD / shop / production drawing storage or version control
  • Sample tracking, design freeze workflow
  • Site measurement records as a structured entity

Recommendation

Don't build a CAD tool — integrate one. A simple file-attachment field (drawing version, uploaded-by, uploaded-at) on Projects and Quotations, backed by Supabase Storage, would close 70% of this gap without competing with SketchUp/AutoCAD/Pinterest-style tools the design team already uses.

Technical Architecture Review

This is the section that matters most for investor and acquirer diligence, and it needs the most direct language.

LayerCurrent stateRisk

BackendSupabase (Postgres + Auth + REST) — a solid managed choice for this stage.Low

What this means in plain terms

The architecture is right for what it is: a fast-moving internal tool for one team, built and shipped by one operator. It is not, today, an architecture a technical acquirer or Series A investor would sign off on without a re-platforming commitment — the single-file frontend and denormalized data model are the two items that will come up first in any serious technical diligence.

SWOT Analysis

Strengths

  • Purpose-built parametric costing engine — real, defensible IP
  • Genuinely end-to-end operational coverage, single source of truth
  • CEO-editable role/permission system
  • Fast, low-cost iteration — five modules shipped in one cycle
  • Strong referral-channel attribution, unusual for this tool class

Weaknesses

  • No client-facing surface anywhere in the product
  • Denormalized data model limits reporting depth
  • No automated tests, no staging environment
  • Zero marketing/communication automation (WhatsApp, email, ads)
  • Single-tenant architecture blocks multi-branch growth

Opportunities

  • Client portal could become a genuine differentiator in this market
  • The costing engine is strong enough to license/white-label to peer firms
  • AI-assisted quoting is a natural, low-effort extension of existing data
  • WhatsApp-first workflows fit this industry's actual client behavior

Threats

  • Key-person/single-developer dependency
  • A better-funded competitor building a similar vertical tool with a proper client portal
  • Compliance exposure (GST/audit-readiness) surfacing at the worst time — mid-fundraise or mid-audit
  • Scale outpacing the current architecture faster than re-platforming can happen

Final CEO Report

A concise version of everything above, written for the owner's desk.

Overall business health: Operationally solid, financially under-instrumented. The company is running real projects through a real system, end to end — that alone puts it ahead of most peers still on spreadsheets and WhatsApp groups. The gap is not in operations; it's in the layers that touch money-at-audit-grade and the customer directly.

ERP & digital maturity: Mid-stage. The system covers more ground than most competitors' tools in this price bracket, built at a pace ({7 modules across one week of focused work}) that a typical vendor RFP process wouldn't match in a quarter. It is not yet enterprise-grade underneath, and shouldn't be marketed or diligenced as such without the Phase 3–4 work.

Strengths to protect: The costing engine and the CEO-editable permission system are genuinely rare at this scale — don't let a future re-platforming project accidentally throw either away.

Critical weaknesses to fix first: No client-facing surface, no WhatsApp/communication automation, and no audit-grade financial ledger. These three are the ones a customer, a lender, or an investor will notice first.

Immediate priorities: WhatsApp quote delivery and follow-up reminders (days of effort, real revenue impact), a discount-approval control (hours of effort, real margin protection), and a scheduled backup drill (hours of effort, real risk reduction).

Long-term strategic recommendation: Treat Phase 3–4 (schema normalization, real ledger, multi-branch readiness) as a funded initiative, not an ongoing side-project — it is the work that determines whether this becomes a sellable platform or stays a very good internal tool.

Growth potential after implementing these recommendations: A realistic 25–40% improvement in quote-to-close speed and a materially stronger financial-controls story within 12 months — enough to change the conversation in a genuine fundraising or acquisition process, not just make the team's day-to-day easier.
