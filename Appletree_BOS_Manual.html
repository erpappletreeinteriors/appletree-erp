PART I · 01

Company Organization

This section establishes who Appletree Interiors is, how it is structured, who reports to whom, what each department owns, what every key role is accountable for, and how far each role can act without escalating. Every later section in this manual — processes, SOPs, RACI, KPIs — refers back to the roles defined here.

1.1Vision, Mission & Values

Vision

To be the most trusted name in bespoke interior fit-out and custom furniture manufacturing — known equally for design integrity, manufacturing precision, and doing exactly what we said we would, on the date we said we would.

Mission

We turn a client's brief into a finished space through disciplined project management, in-house craftsmanship, and transparent communication at every stage — so clients are never guessing what happens next.

Values

  • Craftsmanship — precision is non-negotiable
  • Integrity — say the true cost, the true date, the true problem
  • Ownership — every task has one accountable owner
  • Client-centricity — the client hears from us before they have to ask
  • Discipline — no stage skipped, no shortcut on safety or quality

1.2Organizational Structure

This mirrors how work actually flows: Commercial wins and defines the job, Delivery builds and installs it, Enabling Functions keep the company solvent, staffed, and the client cared for after handover.

Escalation rule

If a decision sits above your limit, it is escalated the same working day — it is never held pending "until the approver is free." Delayed escalation is treated the same as an unauthorized approval.

2.1

Lead Generation

Responsible: Marketing Lead · Supporting: Sales & BD, Referral Partners

Generate a steady flow of qualified prospects matching Appletree's target client profile.

Scope

From first marketing touch (ad, referral, website, walk-in) to a logged, assigned lead in CRM.

Inputs

Campaigns, website enquiries, referral submissions, walk-ins, exhibitions.

Outputs

Qualified lead record with contact info, source, budget indication, project type.

Workflow
  1. Capture enquiry from any channel into CRM within 2 hours.
  2. Tag lead source; score lead on budget fit, project size, timeline.
  3. Send client an auto-acknowledgement within 30 minutes.
  4. Route lead to an available Sales & BD executive by territory/workload.
  5. Review weekly lead-source performance; reallocate marketing spend.

6.1KPI Principles

  • Every KPI has exactly one accountable owner — the same role marked A for the related activity in §4.1's RACI matrix.

PART III · 09

Quality Management

§2.11 and §3.9 established that QC is structurally independent and holds veto authority. This section goes one level deeper into each of the four inspection points, how snags are tracked to closure, how client feedback re-enters the business, and how recurring problems get fixed at the root rather than patched every time they resurface.

Defect severity classification — used at every inspection point below

CRITICAL Safety, structural, or wrong-material issue — automatic rejection, no discretion. MAJOR Visible defect affecting finish or function — affected units rejected/reworked. MINOR Cosmetic, within tolerance — QC Manager discretion to accept, reject, or accept with a cost adjustment.

9.1Incoming Material Inspection

Objective: verify every incoming shipment matches the PO specification and quality standard before it enters usable stock.

Responsible

QC Inspector (reports to QC Manager), supported by Inventory at receiving.

Sampling Method

100% quantity count; visual/dimensional check on a sample (minimum 5 units or 10% of the batch, whichever is greater) for standard items; 100% inspection for critical or visible-finish materials.

Pass/Fail Protocol

Critical → full batch rejected, vendor notified same-day, payment held. Major → affected units quarantined, vendor debit note raised. Minor → QC Manager discretion.

Inspection Criteria
  • Quantity match to PO and Delivery Note
  • Material grade/specification match to the approved vendor sample or Material Selection Sheet
  • Visual/surface defect check — scratches, warping, moisture damage, colour variation
  • Dimensional check on sampled pieces
  • Certification/compliance documents where applicable (e.g., fire rating, timber certification)
Documentation

Objective: catch defects at the earliest possible operation, before further value is added on top of a flawed component.

9.7Corrective and Preventive Action (CAPA)

Objective: ensure a defect or complaint that recurs is fixed at its root cause, not repeatedly patched at the symptom level.

Responsible

QC Manager owns the CAPA process end-to-end; the relevant Department Head owns implementing the corrective action.

Root Cause Method

QC Manager facilitates with the relevant department, using "5 Whys" or fishbone analysis as the default method.

Effectiveness Check

QC Manager confirms the defect has not recurred within a defined verification window (e.g., one quarter) before the CAPA is closed.

Mandatory Trigger Criteria
  • Any defect recurring 2+ times in a quarter (§2.11, §3.9)
  • Any warranty issue recurring across multiple clients (§2.16, §3.13)
  • Any customer complaint theme recurring (§9.6)
  • Any P1 safety incident (§4.5)
  • Any vendor quality failure recurring 2+ times (§3.6)
Process
  1. Log the CAPA in the CAPA Register with a unique ID, trigger reference, and date opened.
  2. Conduct root cause analysis with the relevant department.
  3. Take corrective action — fix the current instance.
  4. Define preventive action — the process, SOP, checklist, or training change that stops recurrence (may route through Continuous Improvement, §12, as an SOP amendment).
  5. Implement with a named owner and due date.
  6. Run the effectiveness check before closing the CAPA — closing immediately after the corrective action, without confirming it worked, is a process violation.
Documentation
CAPA FormCAPA RegisterLinked Defect Log / Service Request / Risk Register entries

Standing Reporting

CAPA status is a standing agenda item at the Monthly Management Review (§8.5) and a trend-review item at the Quarterly Business Review (§8.6).

Escalation

A CAPA with no assigned owner, or with no movement for 30 days, escalates to the CEO as a P2 item (§4.5).

10.3Business Continuity Principles

The same four contingency patterns recur across almost every department above — worth stating once as standing policy rather than re-deriving per risk.

Redundancy, buffer, insurance, documentation

  • Redundancy — no critical role, vendor, or machine is a true single point of failure; every one has a named backup or minimum-two-source policy.
  • Buffer — schedule buffer on critical-path work, safety stock on long-lead materials, contingency % in every Cost Sheet, and a working capital line for cash timing gaps.
  • Insurance — site, product liability, and key-person coverage sized to the company's actual exposure, reviewed annually with the Quarterly Business Review (§8.6).
  • Documentation — this manual itself is the primary continuity control: a documented SOP means a role's work continues correctly even if the person filling it changes.

10.4Risk Escalation

12.1SOP Review & Amendment Process

  • Employee suggestion — approved via §12.6.

  4. Version increment — the section is updated, dated, and logged, following §5.1's naming and versioning discipline.

12.3Root Cause Analysis

Objective: give the company one standard method for reaching the real cause of a problem, so CAPA outcomes (§9.7) are comparable and defensible across departments.

Standard Method

"5 Whys" as the default — ask "why" until the true root cause is reached. A fishbone/cause-and-effect diagram for complex, multi-factor problems with several plausible contributing causes.

Worked Example

Problem: a batch of cabinet doors failed final QC for finish defects.

  1. Why did the doors fail QC? — Paint finish had visible brush marks.
  2. Why did the finish have brush marks? — Sprayed under incorrect booth humidity.
  3. Why was the booth humidity incorrect? — Humidity control wasn't checked before the shift.
  4. Why wasn't it checked? — No item for it on the Machine Pre-Start Checklist.
  5. Why isn't it on the checklist? — The checklist predates the spray booth's installation and was never updated.

Root Cause

A checklist gap, not operator error — the preventive action is a checklist update (§9.2, routed through §12.1), not retraining the operator.

Documents
Root Cause Analysis Worksheet (attached to the CAPA Form, §9.7)

Quality Standard

Analysis stops at a cause the company can actually act on ("the checklist was outdated") — never at a cause that assigns blame without a fix ("the operator wasn't careful enough").

Escalation

An analysis that cannot identify an actionable cause after reasonable effort escalates to the CEO for a decision on how to proceed.

12.4Lessons Learned

Objective: capture what worked and what didn't on every project, before the people who know it are on the next job and it's forgotten.

Process
  1. At Gate 6 (Project Closure), the Project Manager completes a Lessons-Learned entry — required before the gate passes (§7.2).
  2. Structured prompts: what went well (repeat this)? what didn't (avoid this)? what would we do differently? any recurring theme with prior projects?
  3. Entries are logged and tagged by project type/department for pattern-spotting.
  4. Reviewed as a set — not project-by-project — at the Quarterly Business Review (§8.6), since patterns across projects are the real signal.
  5. A pattern suggesting an SOP change routes through §12.1; one suggesting a training gap routes through §11.3.
Documents
Lessons-Learned Log entry (per project)Quarterly Lessons-Learned Summary

Quality Standard

An entry names something specific and actionable — not "communication could be better," but what specifically, on what date, with what fix.

12.6Employee Suggestion System

Objective: give every employee — not just Department Heads — a direct channel to improve how the company runs, so a good idea never dies just because no one asked.

Process
  1. Any employee submits a suggestion — the problem, the idea, the expected benefit — to their Department Head or directly to HR.
  2. Department Head provides an initial feasibility view within 2 weeks.
  3. Viable suggestions enter the Process Improvement loop (§12.5) as a pilot candidate.
  4. The outcome — approved and piloted, or explained why not — is always communicated back to the employee. A suggestion is never left unanswered.
  5. Suggestions leading to an implemented, measurable improvement are eligible for Rewards & Recognition (§11.7).

Quality Standard

Every suggestion gets a documented response — accepted, piloted, or declined with a reason — within a defined window. Silence is treated as a process failure, not an acceptable outcome.

Escalation

A suggestion unanswered beyond the defined window escalates to HR for follow-up. An employee who believes their suggestion was dismissed unfairly can raise it directly with the CEO.
